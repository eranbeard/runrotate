Who Should Buy the Adidas Adizero Adios Pro 4?

Runners chasing PRs from 5K to marathon - If you’re aiming to hit a new personal best, this shoe gives you the energy return, lightweight build, and race-day feel to help get you there. Experienced runners who prefer a soft-yet-springy super shoe - It’s ideal if you want bounce without the harshness or instability of a stiff carbon plate. Those who found more aggressive super shoes too unstable - The Adios Pro 4 offers a smoother ride and a more grounded feel with similar speed benefits. Neutral runners with a midfoot or forefoot strike - The shoe performs best when you’re moving fast and landing efficiently. Runners wanting durability in a premium racing shoe - If you like to get more than a few races out of your super shoes, the Adios Pro 4 holds up impressively well. Lightweight, performance-focused runners - Its snug fit and aggressive geometry cater best to those with a natural stride and narrow to medium-width feet. It’s not the best choice for heavy heel strikers, overpronators, or casual joggers looking for a soft daily trainer—but if you’re serious about racing fast, this shoe absolutely delivers.

Fit & Feel

The fit of the Adios Pro 4 is what I’d call a secure “race-day” fit, with a snug wrap around the foot. The upper is an ultra-thin engineered mesh (Adidas calls it Micro Fit) that’s almost translucent and extremely lightweight. When I first put the shoes on, the upper felt luxuriously soft against my foot - a huge improvement from some past Adizero models that had stiffer uppers.. It comfortably conforms to my foot shape without any bulky padding. Lockdown is excellent: the shoe has a new lacing system that is easy to dial in, plus a lightly padded, gusseted tongue that prevents any lace bite or top-of-foot pressure￼. Ibarely have to cinch the laces very tight to get a reliable hold - there’s an integrated LIGHTLOCK band and a “Slinglaunch” heel piece that together keep my midfoot and heel in place. The only slight downside is that the laces aren’t long enough to double knot when I use heel lock lacing. Despite there being no traditional rigid heel counter, my heel feels nicely cupped with no slippage at all (even when cornering or doing speedwork). The toe box is on the narrow/tapered side, typical of a racing shoe - my toes don’t splay out much, but I haven’t had blister issues. Runners with wide feet might find the toebox a bit tight , but for me (a medium-width foot) it’s just about right, though definitely performance-snug. The shoe does run a little short in length - I went true to size for 5K/10K use and it’s fine, but if I were aiming to race a full marathon in these, I’d consider going a half size up for extra toe room (a common suggestion with this model)￼. Overall, the Adios Pro 4 fits like a racing glove: very secure and ready for speed, yet surprisingly comfortable. I even felt confident enough to wear thinner socks on race day because the interior is smooth with no rough hotspots.

Cushioning

The Adios Pro 4’s cushioning hits a sweet spot between softness and responsiveness. Right from the first run, the midsole felt plush and shock-absorbing without being mushy. Adidas uses their latest Lightstrike Pro foam, and it’s noticeably softer and bouncier than before￼. Every footstrike is greeted with a forgiving compressive feel that spares my legs on pavement pounding, yet there’s an energetic rebound that propels me forward. In my 5K and 10K runs, the impact protection was excellent - I never felt the road poking through despite the shoe’s ultralight vibe. During the longer City2Surf event (with its mix of flats and hills), the cushioning held up impressively; my feet and knees didn’t feel beat up at all by the end. It’s worth noting the 6 mm drop (39 mm heel, 33 mm forefoot) which contributes to a smooth, rolling transition and keeps the shoe feeling natural underfoot. Overall, the Adios Pro 4 delivers a soft-yet-springy ride that’s more forgiving than some firmer “super shoes.” In fact, it reminds me of a slightly softer Nike Vaporfly in feel, but with a bit more bounce on toe-off￼. Importantly, I also didn’t experience the calf strain I sometimes got in the Nike Alphafly 3, perhaps because the Adidas foam and EnergyRods (instead of a stiff full carbon plate) work together to reduce harsh pounding on the lower legs. After races, my muscles feel surprisingly fresh, indicating the cushioning is doing its shock-absorbing job well.

Responsiveness

When it comes to responsiveness, the Adios Pro 4 delivers a lively, engaging ride. By responsiveness, I mean how much “pop” or energy return you feel with each stride - and this shoe has plenty. The combination of that bouncy Lightstrike Pro foam and the carbon EnergyRods 2.0 (five rods embedded through the midsole) creates a noticeable spring effect. Especially as you pick up the pace, you can feel the shoe storing energy and then releasing it as you toe-off. During interval workouts and fast finishes, I loved how the Adios Pro 4 seemed to come alive - the faster I went, the more it seemed to want to give back. In one 5K race, I found an extra gear in the final kilometer; I consciously pushed off harder and the shoe responded with an energizing bounce that propelled me forward. It’s a very satisfying feeling, almost like the shoe has a little trampoline under the forefoot. That responsiveness helped me hit some personal best splits without feeling like I was straining - it’s as if the Adios Pro 4 rewards you for running faster by making it easier to do so. What’s interesting is that the energy return is there without the harsh rigidity that some carbon-plated shoes have. The carbon rods in the Adidas are slightly more flexible than a full-length plate, so while they still provide a snappy toe-off, they don’t feel overly stiff. As a result, I get a nice balance of cushion and pop. There’s a smooth roll through my gait thanks to the rocker shape, and then a quick toe spring that doesn’t slap the ground loudly (bonus: the Adios Pro 4 is a quiet shoe - unlike the Alphafly 3 which clacked on the road and could be a bit annoying without music). I should note, at easier paces the responsiveness is less pronounced - in slow jogs, the shoe can feel a bit flat or like it’s waiting for you to push it. It really shines when you go tempo or faster. In my half-marathon-pace workouts, the shoe felt like it had a “cruise control” effect, locking in a fast rhythm and helping me maintain it with minimal effort. And in short races (5K/10K), it absolutely has that high-performance “pop” comparable to other top-tier racers. I’d say the Adios Pro 4’s responsiveness rivals any shoe out there right now - it’s on par with Nike’s ZoomX shoes in giving energy return, just delivered in a slightly different (smoother) manner. When I really needed a kick on a steep uphill during City2Surf, I sprinted and felt the shoe spring me up the climb. It’s the kind of responsiveness that can help you eke out a PR or finish strong, which is exactly what I look for in a racing shoe.

Traction

Adidas nailed it with the traction on this shoe. The outsole features a combination of their new Lightstrike/Lightraxion GCPU rubber compound throughout and a patch of Continental™ rubber at the toe-off area￼. In plain terms: the grip is fantastic. I’ve run with the Adios Pro 4 on a variety of surfaces – dry asphalt, wet roads after rain, and even a bit of slick boardwalk, and I never once felt a slip. Pushing through corners at speed, the shoe holds the ground confidently. During a recent 10K with some sharp turns and a wet drink station zone, I was impressed that I could dig into turns and accelerate without losing footing; the Continental rubber up front really bites the pavement . The tread pattern isn’t aggressive like a trail shoe (it’s mostly flat with small rectangular lugs), but it seems engineered for road racing traction. Even on a rainy morning Parkrun, the outsole gave me a trustworthy grip, inspiring confidence to maintain my pace. Another perk: after ~100 km on them so far, the outsole shows almost no wear, which is impressive for a super shoe with soft foam￼. Other runners have reported putting 400-500 km on these with minimal outsole damage￼, which is outstanding for a high-end racer. In short, the Adios Pro 4 grips like a champ and doesn’t burn out quickly, making it suitable not just for race day but also fast training runs where you want that secure traction.

Stability

Stability is often a concern with super shoes due to their tall, cushy soles, and indeed the Adios Pro 4 sits on a high stack. I found the stability to be decent given the height and softness, but it still has its caveats. At faster paces with a midfoot or forefoot strike, the shoe actually feels quite stable and planted, likely thanks to the broad forefoot platform and those carbon EnergyRods acting like stabilizing fingers within the foam. In my 5K tempo runs and interval sessions, I never felt wobbly; the ride was smooth and I could trust my footing. The wide forefoot helps prevent excessive side-to-side roll, and overall it’s less tippy than some other max-stack racers I’ve tried. For example, compared to my Nike Alphafly 3, the Adios Pro 4 feels more natural and less “blocky” underfoot - the Alphafly’s super-tall air pods made me feel like I was on stilts at times (which isn’t great when you’re already 6ft 4”!), whereas the Adios Pro 4 has a bit more ground connection ￼. I also appreciate that the Adidas platform doesn’t overly force my foot - with the Alphafly’s carbon plate, I sometimes felt unstable and even got some ankle wobble on uneven ground. The Adios Pro 4 in contrast has a slightly gentler geometry (a rocker starting around 60% of the shoe’s length) which helps keep transitions predictable even as you tire. That said, heel strikers or slower paces might find the stability less impressive. The heel of the shoe has a large cut-out cavity to save weight and add bounce, which means if I land hard on my heels (like during a slow jog or a moment of bad form), the rearfoot can feel a bit squishy and not super stable. The foam compresses a lot at the heel, so there’s a mild sensation of wobble until you roll forward. I noticed on an easy recovery run that it was trickier to keep a perfectly steady, straight path - the shoe kind of encourages you to get up on your forefoot and go (it definitely performs best when you do). This also makes the Adios Pro 4 feel odd to walk in. So, while the Adios Pro 4 isn’t a stability shoe by any means, it’s manageable for a neutral runner like me at training and racing speeds. If you severely overpronate or land on your heels heavily, you might find it unstable in the rearfoot, but for my stride, it’s been fine - I felt more secure in these than in the Alphafly 3, and even on slightly uneven city streets during City2Surf I had no ankle rolls. In summary, stability is adequate for a high-stack racer: great when you’re running fast and on form, slightly shaky if you’re not. I’d just advise being mindful on tight turns or when running slowly in these, as the bouncy foam can introduce a bit of lateral give.

Breathability & Temperature Control

With such a thin mesh upper, you’d expect the Adios Pro 4 to be ultra breathable… and it is quite breathable, but not without some quirks. The upper is a single-layer engineered mesh that you can literally see through in places. This lets a fair amount of air flow in, which I appreciated during hot runs here on the Gold Coast. On my summer Parkruns, I didn’t feel my feet overheating significantly - the shoe’s minimal upper material doesn’t trap much heat. In fact, the interior is almost sock-like with how light and airy it feels initially. Ventilation is generally good, especially compared to the previous Adios Pro version which had a thicker mesh. Adidas even lined the upper with that LIGHTLOCK support band in a way that doesn’t seem to block airflow too much, and there are plenty of tiny perforations. However, I did notice that on longer runs (or particularly sweaty efforts) the shoes would end up pretty damp on the inside. The mesh, while breathable, also tends to absorb and hold moisture. By the end of my 14 km City2Surf, my socks were quite wet - the upper had soaked up sweat rather than evaporating it instantly. So in practice the ventilation might not feel as effective in keeping your feet dry. It’s not that the shoe runs hot (I wasn’t overheating), but rather once moisture is in, the thin material gets swampy. This could partly be because the tongue and collar, though minimal, can absorb sweat too, and there’s no thick insole to soak it up (the insole is very thin). Also, the snug fit means not a ton of air is circulating inside when your foot is locked in. I’d say breathability is average overall - good airflow when moving, but it doesn’t actively keep you cool or dry in extreme conditions. Some reviewers have noted the upper actually lacks breathability despite being thin￼, likely referencing this sweat issue. In my personal use, my feet never got overheated to the point of discomfort, but I did feel some sweat in the shoe afterwards. For most runs this is a minor issue; just be sure to air them out properly. If you race in very hot climates, you might wish for an upper that wicks moisture better, but for temperate conditions the Adios Pro 4’s breathability is sufficient. On balance, I’ll take the super light mesh (and just deal with a bit of moisture) over a heavier, more padded upper any day, since it contributes to the shoe’s fast feel.

Build Quality

The build quality of the Adios Pro 4 is impressive, befitting a flagship racing shoe from Adidas. From top to bottom, you can tell a lot of thought went into materials and construction. Starting with the midsole and propulsion tech: the shoe has two layers of Lightstrike Pro foam with a set of full-length carbon EnergyRods 2.0 sandwiched in between. These rods are actually visible if you look at the bottom of the shoe (through the cutouts). Everything in this midsole complex feels well put together - there’s no weird flexing or asymmetry, and the rods are positioned to mirror your metatarsal bones, which indicates precision engineering. When running, I don’t feel any inconsistencies underfoot (some poorly made carbon shoes can have “dead spots” or one rod/plate corner poking oddly - not the case here). The rocker geometry is also seamlessly integrated; the shoe has a smooth curvature that transitions nicely, indicating the design and manufacturing nailed the shape they intended. The fact that the midsole foam has maintained its integrity over many miles (no significant compression set or warping) speaks to its quality. Moving to the outsole, Adidas used a dual-compound approach: the majority of the outsole is their new Lighttraxion GCPU rubber, and there’s a Continental rubber segment at the toe for extra durability and grip. Both are holding up excellently. The rubber seems securely bonded to the foam - I see no peeling edges or glue issues. The patterns are cut cleanly and evenly. Even the exposed foam portions (in the midfoot cutout) haven’t scuffed or chipped much. It’s clear that Adidas prioritized making a racing shoe that’s tough enough for real-world conditions, and the craftsmanship reflects that. I also like the little details, like the embedded torsion plate (if you peek into the midfoot cavity, there’s a small stabilizing piece - maybe just the rods visible, but it looks cool) and the neat finish of the outsole edges. The upper construction is another highlight. The material is a one-way stretch mesh that Adidas calls Micro Fit. It’s very soft to the touch but surprisingly strong. The seams (what few there are) are well done - for instance, the tongue is lightly sewn in (gusseted) and that stitching is uniform and low-profile so it doesn’t rub. The Lightlock band that provides midfoot lockdown is integrated between layers of the upper in a way that you don’t see any harsh edges or glue marks; you just feel the support. The lacing holes are reinforced subtly, and the thin laces themselves are high quality (they’ve never come undone on me mid-run, which I can’t say for all shoes - these have a slight texture that helps them stay tied). Another neat build feature is the Slinglaunch heel design: instead of a chunky heel counter, Adidas used a flexible heel wrap with a slight external overlay for structure. It’s very well executed - my heel feels secure but there’s no rigid plastic to cause irritation. The padding around the collar is minimal but placed exactly where needed (just at the Achilles area), showing attention to detail. Even the heel pull tab is cleverly made: it’s a thin fabric loop (keeps weight down) that has a reflective patch and apparently a hidden message printed on the inside, a fun little Easter egg from Adidas. All the elements of the upper are glued and assembled with precision - I see no excess glue or misalignment. The overall feel is that of a very premium, well-constructed shoe. Having used the Adios Pro 4 extensively, I’ve encountered zero issues like tearing fabric, detached soles, or malfunctioning components. The quality control on my pair was top-notch. This gives me confidence in the shoe’s longevity and in Adidas’s craftsmanship. It’s also worth noting I didn’t experience any factory defects (I know earlier Adios Pro versions had occasional complaints of, say, plate rattling or upper ripping - nothing like that here). The build quality contributes to why I trust this shoe on race day; I know it’s not going to fall apart or perform inconsistently. In summary, Adidas delivered excellent build quality with the Adios Pro 4 - everything from the cutting-edge foam to the outsole to the featherlight upper is executed in a way that feels solid and reliable, despite the shoe’s minimal weight. For a shoe that I’m pushing to high speeds, that peace of mind is invaluable.

Durability

One area that truly surprised me is durability. Super shoes are notorious for wearing out quickly, but the Adios Pro 4 seems built to last (relatively speaking). Firstly, the outsole durability is exceptional. As mentioned, after significant mileage my pair’s outsole rubber has barely any visible wear - it’s just a little scuffed and dirty, but none of the rubber patches have worn through￼. The new Lighttraxion outsole compound that Adidas uses is impressively tough; some runners have even called it “indestructible” after hundreds of kilometers. I’ve read reports of folks doing 400-600 km in these shoes and still getting great traction and performance. That means you won’t need to retire them after just a couple of races (which helps justify the premium price). The midsole foam has also held up well in my experience, with the Lightstrike Pro foam still feeling energetic and cushioned after 200kms - maybe slightly less bouncy than day one, but nothing significant. It hasn’t bottomed out or become lifeless at all. I noted only minimal creasing in the midsole sidewalls, and the ride remains as fun as it was initially. This aligns with others’ feedback: even after a full marathon or many long runs, the foam maintains its performance￼. In practical terms, I expect to get many races and fast training sessions out of these without feeling a drop-off in cushioning or speed. The upper is often a durability weak point in ultralight shoes, but so far so good. The fabric is incredibly thin, yet it’s showing no rips or significant fraying in my pair. The only sign of wear is cosmetic: because my colorway is very light (white with grey/orange accents), it has picked up some dirt, though the speckled “dirt splatter” design on my Chalk White/Grey Strata/Impact Orange version does a great job camouflaging actual dirt. The upper does absorb sweat (more on that under Breathability), so over time it can get a bit discolored or musky if not dried properly. However, structurally it’s holding up, with all the glued overlays and the stitching around the tongue are intact and secure. The build quality of the shoe, from the bonding of the carbon rods inside to the attachment of outsole rubber feels premium. I haven’t experienced any separation or glue failures. All in all, the Adios Pro 4 is one of the most durable racing shoes I’ve owned. I feel confident using it not just for races but also for plenty of fast workouts, knowing it can handle the mileage. If durability was a concern, this shoe eases that worry - it’s definitely built to go the distance.

Style & Design

I know looks are subjective, but I have to talk about the visual appeal of the Adios Pro 4 - because these are some slick-looking shoes. My pair is the Chalk White/Grey Strata/Impact Orange colorway, which is a mostly off-white shoe with gray accents and bright orange hits (plus that unique “dirt splatter” effect printed on the midsole). At first I wasn’t sure about the splatter design - it literally looks like I ran through a mud puddle out of the box! - but it’s grown on me. It gives the shoe some character and, as a bonus, it actually hides real dirt or scuffs pretty well. After a few uses, they more or less look the same as when new, since any grime just blends into the intentional speckled pattern. The overall silhouette of the Adios Pro 4 is sleek and modern. It has a racer’s profile with a high stack and a noticeable curve to the sole, but the design lines are clean. The signature three stripes are integrated as transparent overlays that kind of fade into the midsole, which is a very cool visual effect. In fact, the white-based colorway I have might be one of the cleanest-looking racing shoes out there - it’s almost too pretty to get dirty (though obviously I did!). Adidas added a pop of color with an orange (almost neon coral) strip of fabric around the heel collar on my pair, giving it a bit of flair that says “race day” without being over the top. I’ve gotten a few compliments at the start line about these kicks, and I have to agree with other reviewers: the Adios Pro 4 is an objectively great-looking shoe. That said, I’ll confess: I personally preferred the looks of the Nike Alphafly 3 that I owned previously. The Alphafly had that futuristic vibe - super high slab of foam, visible air pods, crazy shapes - which some might find ugly, but I thought it was kind of awesome and distinctive. In comparison, the Adios Pro 4 is a bit more traditional in appearance (no visible gimmicks, just a lot of foam). It’s a different aesthetic: the Adidas comes off as classy and sleek, whereas the Nike screamed high-tech gadgetry. Depending on your taste, you might favor one or the other. For me, the Alphafly 3 looked like a spaceship, and I dig that. However, I’m not at all disappointed with the Adios Pro 4’s style. The more I wear them, the more I appreciate the design details - like the subtle textures in the mesh and the way the midsole is sculpted. Plus, Adidas has released a bunch of colorways (I believe there are around 9+ colors available now), so there’s plenty of choice beyond my “chalk white” version. You can go for a flashy Lucid Lemon yellow, a striking Flash Aqua, classic black, or others. There’s something satisfying about lacing up a shoe that not only feels fast but looks fast. And these definitely look the part of an elite racing shoe. In my race photos, the Adios Pro 4 absolutely pops. So overall, I’m very happy with the looks - I’d give style points for the minimalistic yet eye-catching design. Whether you prefer understated (like the mostly white colorway) or loud (some of the brighter options), Adidas has you covered. And who knows, maybe one day I’ll snag a pair in another color, just because I like them so much!

Value for Money

At a retail price of $250 USD (approximately what I paid), the Adios Pro 4 sits firmly in the premium super-shoe category. It’s a significant investment for a pair of running shoes, so the question is: does it deliver value for money? In my experience, yes - it absolutely earns its keep relative to its price. Here’s why: performance-wise, you’re getting one of the top marathon racing shoes on the market, which can meaningfully aid your speed. The cushioning, weight, and responsiveness are all top-notch (as detailed above), putting it on par with or even above its competitors. I’ve run some of my fastest times in these shoes, which for many runners justifies the cost if you’re chasing PRs. But beyond sheer performance, the Adios Pro 4 offers value in its versatility and durability. This isn’t a one-trick pony that you only pull out for a single race. I’ve been able to use it for everything from short 5K races to longer runs, and it excels across that spectrum. It’s rare for a shoe to feel appropriate for a 5K and a marathon, but I would trust the Adios Pro 4 for either. That means if you buy a pair, you can potentially use it in all your key events rather than needing a different shoe for each distance - a good bang-for-buck consideration. Durability also boosts its value: as noted, you can expect to log a lot of miles in these. Many super shoes are “done” after ~100-200 miles, but the Adios Pro 4’s robust outsole and resilient foam suggest you could double that lifespan. Knowing that I can train in them regularly and still race in them makes me feel like I’m really getting my money’s worth. In contrast, with my Alphafly 3, I was hesitant to use them outside of races because I was worried about wearing out the ZoomX foam and the outsole pods quickly (and given their higher ~$275 price, that hurt the value proposition). With the Adios Pro 4, I’m more inclined to lace them up for a fast tempo run or interval session without guilt, since they can handle it. Another aspect is build quality - the materials here are high-grade (Continental rubber, advanced foams, carbon rods, etc.), and the engineering that went into the shoe is evident. You’re paying for cutting-edge tech and it shows. No corners were cut; even small features like the suede-like heel lining or the hidden message on the heel pull tab give it a premium feel. In terms of intangible value, I’ll add that these shoes are fun to run in. They genuinely enhance the running experience for me - and that enjoyment factor is hard to put a price on. If a shoe motivates you to get out and run fast, that’s valuable too! Of course, $250 is still $250 - if you’re a casual runner or on a tight budget, there are more affordable shoes that will get the job done (including older versions of super shoes on discount). But if you’re an enthusiast like me who appreciates the latest gear and wants a top-tier racer/trainer, the Adios Pro 4 is worth the premium. I feel it performs above its price point in several areas: it maintained the same price as the previous model but improved in foam, fit, and outsole. In the realm of super shoes, I’d actually call it a smart buy because you don’t sacrifice durability. Summing up value: the Adios Pro 4 is expensive but delivers. Given its long lifespan and all-around excellence, I’d rate the value as high. It’s an investment in speed and comfort that I haven’t regretted at all.

Final Thoughts

After putting the Adidas Adizero Adios Pro 4 through its paces in various races and runs, I can confidently say it’s one of the best running shoes I’ve ever used. It combines the qualities we runners crave - lightweight, superb cushioning, high energy return, and solid durability - into one sleek package. There are a few minor downsides (a snug fit here, a bit of heel instability there, and the premium price), but they pale in comparison to the benefits this shoe offers on the road. It’s a shoe that can make you feel faster the moment you put it on, and importantly, it carries that feeling through to the finish line with comfort. As an enthusiast runner, I appreciate that the Adios Pro 4 isn’t just hype - it genuinely enhanced my running experience, from shaving seconds off my 5K, to keeping my legs fresher in a 10K, to giving me confidence during a big city race. Adidas set out to create a marathon-capable super shoe that’s also friendly enough for everyday runners, and I believe they succeeded. The improvements over past versions (softer foam, better upper, great traction) are noticeable and meaningful. It even addressed some issues I had with other super shoes (like the Alphafly’s noise and instability) by offering a quieter, more stable ride without sacrificing speed. Overall, I’d rate the Adios Pro 4 a solid 9 out of 10. It’s as close to a perfect long-distance racing shoe as I’ve encountered. If the toe box were a touch roomier and the price a touch lower, it might be a 10/10, but as it stands, it’s still an amazing piece of running technology. I highly recommend it to any runner looking for a competitive edge or simply a fantastic feeling shoe for fast runs. Whether you’re lining up for a local Parkrun or a major city marathon, the Adizero Adios Pro 4 has you covered with speed, comfort, and style in spades. Ultimately, the best praise I can give is that I look forward to lacing these up every single time. They’ve truly earned my enthusiasm. If you’re on the fence and the budget allows, give them a try - you might end up setting new PRs and having a blast doing it, just like I have.
